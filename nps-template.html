Net Promoter Score (NPS) is calculated by subtracting the percentage of Detractors (scores 0-6) from the percentage of Promoters (scores 9-10). Scores range from -100 to +100. An NPS above 0 is generally considered good, above 50 is excellent, and above 70 is world-class. The metric was introduced by Fred Reichheld in a 2003 Harvard Business Review article and co-developed with Bain & Company and Satmetrix. While widely adopted by two-thirds of Fortune 1000 companies, academic research by Keiningham et al. (2007) in the Journal of Marketing found NPS is not universally the best predictor of growth. Eskildsen & Kristensen (2011) demonstrated that the mean of all scores is more statistically stable than the NPS formula, which introduces additional variance through its counting method (Fisher & Kordupleski, 2019). Sources: Reichheld 2003; Keiningham et al. 2007; Eskildsen & Kristensen 2011

⚠ Cross-Cultural NPS: Why Raw Scores Are Not Comparable

These benchmarks reflect primarily US and European data. Raw NPS is not comparable across countries without cultural calibration.

Why scores differ by culture: Harzing (2006) studied 26 countries and found that power distance, collectivism, uncertainty avoidance, and extraversion all significantly influence response patterns. Collectivist cultures (Japan, Korea) score low because respondents are reluctant to risk relationships by recommending, not because they are dissatisfied. International Journal of Cross-Cultural Management

Extreme Response Style (ERS): Beuthner et al. (2018) found Mexican respondents showed extreme response bias while South Korean respondents clustered at midpoints. Hispanic ERS disappeared with 10-point scales. Seth et al. (2016) documented that Japanese and Korean respondents are “ambivalent customers mislabeled as detractors.” IJMR; Asia Marketing Journal

Country averages: Brazil +62, India +51, US/UK ~+30, Netherlands +2, Japan -52. A “European NPS” adaptation shifts the Promoter threshold to 8+ based on purchase data. XM Institute; Kimura 2022; KIRC Foundation

Survey Design

NPS Follow-Up Questions: The Research-Backed Approach

Academic research shows open-text follow-ups alone miss what actually drives scores. The optimal approach combines closed-ended driver questions with optional open text.

Why Closed-Ended Drivers + Open Text

The problem with open text alone: Bettencourt & Houston (2024) published in the International Journal of Market Research that open-ended comments have only low-to-moderate convergence with what actually drives customers’ NPS scores. Customers write about salient recent experiences, not the structural factors that determine their recommendation intent.

Bettencourt & Houston (2024). IJMR.

The solution: Supplement open-text with closed-ended driver chips (select 1-3 reasons). This captures the structural drivers that open text misses. Make open text optional — Hadler (2025) in Sociological Methods & Research found that mandatory open-ended probes increase survey breakoff and backtracking.

Hadler (2025). Sociological Methods & Research.

Step 2: Closed-Ended Driver Questions (by Segment)

Present 5-6 selectable options. Respondent picks 1-3. Options change based on score segment. Preston & Colman (2000): 5-7 options is the psychometric sweet spot.

Research

NPS Methodology: What the Research Actually Says

A balanced look at NPS — its origins, academic limitations, and why it still matters despite criticism. With 52 peer-reviewed sources.

The Origins

NPS was introduced by Fred Reichheld in a 2003 Harvard Business Review article, “The One Number You Need to Grow,” and co-developed with Bain & Company and Satmetrix. By 2020, two-thirds of Fortune 1000 companies had adopted NPS. In 2021, Reichheld published “Net Promoter 3.0” in HBR, acknowledging that NPS had been “gamed and misused” and introducing the Earned Growth Rate — an auditable financial metric based on revenue from returning customers and referrals — as a complement to self-reported NPS. HBR 2003; HBR 2021

What the Academic Literature Says

Not the best predictor of growth: Keiningham et al. (2007) in the Journal of Marketing failed to replicate Reichheld’s claims. The American Customer Satisfaction Index outperformed NPS in 5 of 9 industries. Morgan & Rego (2006) in Marketing Science found average satisfaction has the greatest predictive value for business performance, while recommendation-based metrics had “little or no predictive value.” JM 2007; Marketing Science 2006

The 11-point scale exceeds the optimum: Preston & Colman (2000) in Acta Psychologica found psychometric reliability plateaus at 5-7 points and decreases beyond 10. The NPS 0-10 scale has 11 points — more than the statistical optimum. However, changing the scale would break comparability with 20+ years of benchmark data. Acta Psychologica 2000

The question is double-barreled: Schlosser (2024) in the International Journal of Market Research noted the NPS question asks about “friend OR colleague” simultaneously, ignores propensity for negative word-of-mouth, and ignores online WOM to strangers. IJMR 2024

Intention â‰  behavior: Kumar et al. (2007) found 68% of self-reported Promoters never actually make a referral. Romaniuk, Nguyen & East (2011) in IJMR showed respondents are better at predicting when they won’t recommend than when they will. Stated intention does not accurately predict actual behavior. Kumar 2007; IJMR 2011

Single-item limitations: Diamantopoulos et al. (2012) in the Journal of the Academy of Marketing Science found that under most practical conditions, multi-item scales clearly outperform single-item measures. JAMS 2012

Cultural bias: Liked companies score -47 NPS in Japan vs +64 in India (XM Institute, n=17,509). Harzing (2006) showed collectivism and power distance systematically skew response patterns across 26 countries. XM Institute; IJCCM 2006

Why NPS Still Matters

Simplicity drives adoption. More complex metrics (ACSI, CES, NEV) have lower organizational buy-in. NPS gets executive attention precisely because it’s one number.

Trending over time is valuable. Even if the absolute score is imperfect, tracking NPS quarterly reveals meaningful patterns — especially when segmented by cohort.

The follow-up data is the real insight. The open-text “why” and closed-ended drivers generate more actionable data than the numerical score. The survey flow matters more than the formula.

Use it alongside other metrics. De Haan, Verhoef & Wiesel (2015) studied 93 firms across 18 industries and found that combining NPS + CSAT + CES improves prediction of business outcomes vs. any single metric. Int. J. of Research in Marketing 2015

While NPS is the most widely adopted customer loyalty metric, academic research has identified important limitations. Keiningham et al. (2007) in the Journal of Marketing failed to replicate NPS superiority claims, finding ACSI outperformed NPS in five of nine industries. Morgan & Rego (2006) in Marketing Science found average satisfaction has greater predictive value. Preston & Colman (2000) showed the 11-point scale exceeds the psychometric optimum of 5-7 points. Kumar et al. (2007) documented that 68% of self-reported Promoters never made a referral. Schlosser (2024) identified the NPS question as double-barreled. Cultural differences are dramatic — Harzing (2006) showed collectivism and power distance systematically skew responses across 26 countries. Despite these criticisms, NPS remains valuable because its simplicity drives organizational adoption, longitudinal trending reveals meaningful patterns, and De Haan et al. (2015) showed combining NPS with CSAT and CES improves prediction. Sources: Keiningham 2007; Morgan & Rego 2006; Preston & Colman 2000; Kumar 2007; Schlosser 2024; Harzing 2006; De Haan et al. 2015

Survey Design Tips from Academic Research

NPS placed as question #1 gets higher completion. Thau (2021) in Public Administration and van de Walle & van Ryzin (2011) found that asking attribute questions before the overall recommendation question depresses the overall score. Public Administration 2021

Use radio buttons, not sliders. Bosch et al. (2019) in Social Science Computer Review found no quality advantage for sliders. Funke et al. (2011) showed sliders negatively affect response rates, especially on mobile. SSCR 2019; Funke 2011

No progress bars. Liu & Wronski (2018) in Social Science Computer Review found that across 25,000+ surveys, those without progress bars had higher completion rates. SSCR 2018

Survey fatigue follows a linear decline: 68% → 58% → 46% response rate across three surveys in 90 days. Brosnan et al. (2021) in IJMR found reducing items increases engagement and accuracy. Brosnan et al. 2021

Pitfalls

10 Common NPS Mistakes

Research-backed pitfalls that skew your NPS data — with academic citations and fixes.

#1

Surveying Too Frequently

Response rates drop 40% after the 3rd survey in 90 days. Brosnan et al. (2021) in IJMR confirmed that reducing survey frequency increases engagement and accuracy. Brosnan et al. 2021

The Fix

Relationship NPS: maximum quarterly. Transactional: only after key events. Use Formbricks’ response cooldown to enforce automatically.

#2

Not Segmenting Results

A single NPS hides 2-3x variation between segments. De Haan et al. (2015) showed combining segmented metrics improves prediction. Int. J. Research in Marketing 2015

The Fix

Segment by plan tier, customer tenure, use case, and geography. Track each segment’s NPS independently.

#3

Ignoring Passives (7-8)

Passives have 2x the lifetime value of detractors. Eskildsen & Kristensen (2011) noted the NPS formula loses information by collapsing scores into three categories — a 6 and a 0 are treated identically. Eskildsen & Kristensen 2011

The Fix

Ask passives: “What would make you a 9 or 10?” Track mean score alongside NPS to capture passive movements.

#4

Relying on Open Text Alone

Bettencourt & Houston (2024) in IJMR found open-ended comments have only low-to-moderate convergence with what actually drives NPS scores. Bettencourt & Houston 2024

The Fix

Add closed-ended driver chips (select 1-3 reasons) before the open-text follow-up. This captures structural drivers that free text misses.

#5

Wrong Timing & Question Order

Thau (2021) and van de Walle & van Ryzin (2011) showed asking attribute questions before the NPS question depresses the overall score. Monday AM delivers 38% lower response rates vs Tuesday 10am. Public Administration 2021

The Fix

Place NPS as question #1. Send Tuesday-Wednesday, 9am-12pm in the recipient’s timezone.

#6

Not Closing the Loop

Knox & Van Oest (2014) in the Journal of Marketing found the financial benefits of handling complaints outweigh costs. Gelbrich & Roschk (2011) in Journal of Service Research showed recovery effectiveness degrades with time. JM 2014; JSR 2011

The Fix

Auto-alert your team via Slack/email on detractor responses. Set a 48-hour SLA for high-value accounts.

#7

Incentivizing Responses

Offering survey completion incentives inflates NPS by 15-25 points, completely invalidating any benchmarking or trend analysis. Journal of Marketing Research

The Fix

Never incentivize NPS. If response rates are low, fix timing and channel instead.

#8

Using Sliders Instead of Radio Buttons

Funke et al. (2011) found slider scales negatively affect response rates, especially on mobile. Bosch et al. (2019) in Social Science Computer Review found no meaningful quality advantage of sliders over radio buttons. Funke 2011; SSCR 2019

The Fix

Always use radio buttons or clickable number tiles for the 0-10 scale. Never use slider/drag interfaces.

#9

Making Open-Text Follow-Up Mandatory

Hadler (2025) in Sociological Methods & Research found that mandatory open-ended probes increase survey breakoff and backtracking. Forcing respondents to type explanations reduces completion rates. Sociological Methods & Research 2025

The Fix

Always mark open-text follow-ups as (optional). Use closed-ended drivers as the primary diagnostic tool.

#10

Comparing NPS Across Cultures Without Normalization

Harzing (2006) showed power distance, collectivism, and uncertainty avoidance systematically skew NPS across 26 countries. A “good” NPS in Japan (-47 average) means something entirely different than in India (+51 average). IJCCM 2006; XM Institute

The Fix

Never compare raw NPS across countries. Use country-level baselines or cultural calibration. Consider region-specific Promoter thresholds (e.g., European NPS uses 8+ instead of 9+).

B2B Tip: Multi-Stakeholder NPS

In B2B, survey multiple contacts per account — not just your champion. Account-level NPS (average of all contacts) predicts churn 2x better than single-contact NPS.
